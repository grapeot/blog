For five or six years now, I’ve been logging how I spend every single hour of my life. Last year, after accumulating half a decade of data, I decided to feed it all into Big Brother O1 Pro for the first time. I wanted to see what it could tell me about my wins, my losses, and my personality. It was a fascinating experiment—the core of [this article](https://yage.ai/2024-year-end.html) from last year. As 2025 draws to a close, I did the same thing, handing my logs for this year and last to the new Big Brothers—Gemini 3 Pro and GPT 5.2 Pro. Their conclusions left me stunned. On one hand, I had to admit they were right; on the other, I hadn't realized I was doing something so cool and pioneering. Or rather, I had the seeds of these ideas in my mind, but I didn't realize how deep I had already gone in practice.

I’m not sure how many people want to read such a deeply personal summary, but because I found it so inspiring—and because this is a personal blog—I decided to share it. I feel more and more that thinking itself can largely be outsourced in the future, but data remains the bottleneck—or rather, the moat. Without years of detailed time logs, even the most powerful AI couldn't have drawn such interesting conclusions.

### Relationships and Self: Plenty of CPU for Others, Little for the Pure You

Two types of blocks dominate your life:

1.  TT/care: The most stable thread of the year.
2.  AI/Courses/Blogs/Dev: The second thread, growing heavier as the year goes on.

What’s missing:

* Pure time for your partner.
* Pure social time with friends.
* Pure doing nothing, just being alone.

Your life structure looks like this:

* For your child: A high-priority, stable stream of time.
* For work/projects: Almost all remaining high-quality cognitive time.
* For yourself (as an individual): Mostly the scraps left over when you're asleep or exhausted.

It’s not that you don’t love yourself; it’s that in your mind, useful time = time that helps others, the system, or the future.

So:

* You're happy to brainstorm for others, make courses, or share experiences.
* You're happy to spend hours on childcare.
* But you struggle to give yourself two hours for something that doesn't help anyone, doesn't produce content, and doesn't build for the future—something just for you to be comfortable in the moment.

This pays off in the short term: your child has a present parent, your projects move fast, and your reputation grows. But the cost is often deferred: one day, you might wake up and realize you've become a stranger to your own desires and emotions.
